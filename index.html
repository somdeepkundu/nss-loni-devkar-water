The Challenge

💧

Water Stress

Loni Devkar lies in the drought-prone Bhima/Ujani command. Groundwater is over-exploited.

🌾

Sugarcane Dominance

Sugarcane is the primary cash crop but consumes 3x the water of alternatives with lower return per drop.

📊

Low Water Productivity

Current crop mix yields only ~10 Rs/m³. Better crop choices could reach 40–80 Rs/m³.

Crop Water Productivity Framework

Definition

CWP = Crop Output / Water Input

  • Physical: kg/m³ (food per drop)
  • Economic: Rs/m³ (income per drop)

Water Basis

  • Irrigation applied (from surveys)
  • ETc = Evapotranspiration (crop need)
  • Total = rainfall + irrigation

Key Insight

Sugarcane: The dominant crop uses 3× more water than most alternatives but generates only 9.88 Rs/m³.

Grapes & Onion: Lower water input, 4–8× higher income per m³.

Opportunity: Gradual shift to high-value, low-water crops could improve farm income AND reduce groundwater stress.

What the data shows

  • Entirely well-dependent: all 60 ha of irrigated land draws on 61 dug wells + 1 deep tube well. Zero surface-flow or lift schemes.
  • Deeper than its neighbours: Loni Devkar's water table sits at 23 m pre-monsoon vs the Indapur average of 17.4 m — a more stressed aquifer.
  • Thin monsoon recharge: the table recovers only ~2 m after monsoon (23 → 21 m), so wells start each year already deep.
  • No fallback: with no canal or surface backup, a bad monsoon hits the village directly through falling well yields.

Why this reinforces the crop-choice case

Because Loni Devkar pumps 100% of its irrigation from a deep, slowly-recharging aquifer, every cubic metre saved is groundwater kept in the ground. A crop mix that earns more rupees per m³ (grapes, onion) instead of water-guzzling sugarcane is not just an income story here — it is the village's main lever against aquifer depletion.
